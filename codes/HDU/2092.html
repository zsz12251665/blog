This problem is a mathmatical problem. We can use the Vieta's formulas to solve the problem.

According to the Vieta's formulas, if a quadratic equartion which looks like #x#(^2^)-#p##x#+#q#=0 has two solutions of #x#, which may be #x#(_1_) and #x#(_2_), we'll have #x#(_1_)+#x#(_2_)=#p# and #x#(_1_)#x#(_2_)=#q#. So in the problem we're given #p# and #q#. According to the quadratic formula, a quadratic formula which looks like #a##x#(^2^)+#b##x#+#c#=0 have two solutions of #x#, they equal /-#b#±(\#b#(^2^)-4#a##c#\)/2#a#/. That means a quadratic equartion which looks like #x#(^2^)-#p##x#+#q#=0 with two solutions of #x# will have the solutions /#p#±(\#p#(^2^)-4#q#\)/2/. So we can know that if (\#p#(^2^)-4#q#\) is an integer, the two solutions will be integers too. You ask why? If #p# is a odd number, (#p#(^2^)-4#q#) and (\#p#(^2^)-4#q#\) must be odd numbers too. So (#p#±(\#p#(^2^)-4#q#\)) must be even numbers, and the solutions are integers. If #p# is an even number, then (#p#(^2^)-4#q#), (\#p#(^2^)-4#q#\), (#p#±(\#p#(^2^)-4#q#\)) must all be even numbers too. So the solutions are integers. In summary, we can solve the problem by judging if (#p#(^2^)-4#q#) is a square of an integer.
