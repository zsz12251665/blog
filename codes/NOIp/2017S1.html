Solution

I had no idea to solve a pair of (#a#,#b#) while they are as large as 10(^9^) with exGCD!

After getting out of the examination hall, someone told me that the correct answer is (#a##b#-#a#-#b#). How can the problem be a mathmatical one with an O(1) time complexity? Oh Jesus it's just the first problem! It should be solved by simulation or BF! But complaining can change nothing. In the following I will prove you why (#a##b#-#a#-#b#) is the correct answer.

We will use contradiction to prove it. If (#a##b#-#a#-#b#) is able to be formed by #a# and #b#, then we will have #a##b#-#a#-#b#=#a##n#+#b##m# (#n#∈ℕ, #m#∈ℕ). Then it will be transformed into #a##b#=#a#(#n#+1)+#b#(#m#+1). So that we'll have #a#(#b#-#n#-1)=#b#(#m#+1) and #b#(#a#-#m#-1)=#a#(#n#+1), which means #a#|#b#(#m#+1) and #b#|#a#(#n#+1). However, #a# and #b# are coprime, so #a#|(#m#+1) and #b#|(#n#+1). Then we define #m#+1=#x##a# (#x#∈ℕ(_+_)), #n#+1=#y##b# (#y#∈ℕ(_+_)) and put it back to the equation #a##b#=#a#(#n#+1)+#b#(#m#+1), there will be #a##b#=#a#(#n#+1)+#b#(#m#+1) ⇔ #a##b#=#a##b##x#+#a##b##y# ⇔ #x#+#y#=1. Obviously that the sum of two positive integers cannot equal 1, so the hypothesis is wrong and the proposition is proved.
